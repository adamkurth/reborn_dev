Working with crystals¶

A little bit of math¶

Here we briefly outline some conventions assumed in bornagain.

We firstly define the orthogonal coordinates, which we denote as \(\mathbf{r}\). These are the usual “Cartesian” coordinates. The other coordinate system taht is relevant to us is the fractional coordinates, which we denote here as \(\mathbf{x}\). These two vectors are related by the orthogonalization matrix \(\mathbf{O}\) as follows:

\[\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{O}\mathbf{x}\]

The columns of the orthogonalization matrix are the basis vectors \(\mathbf{a}\), \(\mathbf{b}\), \(\mathbf{c}\) of the crystal:

\[\begin{split}\mathbf{O} = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & | \\ \mathbf{a} & \mathbf{b} & \mathbf{c} \\ | & | & | \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

In reciprocal space, we have analogous mathematics for the reciprocal coordinates \(\mathbf{q}\) and fractional Miller indices \(\mathbf{h}\). They are related by the \(\mathbf{A}\) matrix:

\[\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{h}\]

which contains the reciprocal lattice vectors in its columns:

\[\begin{split}\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & | \\ \mathbf{a}^* & \mathbf{b}^* & \mathbf{c}^* \\ | & | & | \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

The reciprocal lattice vectors are defined as

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\mathbf{a}_1^* = \mathbf{a}_2\times \mathbf{a}_3 / V_c\\\mathbf{a}_2^* = \mathbf{a}_3\times \mathbf{a}_1 / V_c\\\mathbf{a}_3^* = \mathbf{a}_1\times \mathbf{a}_2 / V_c\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

where \(V_c = \mathbf{a}_1\cdot(\mathbf{a}_2\times\mathbf{a}_3)\) is the volume of the unit cell.

It is useful to know the following relations:

\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\mathbf{A} = (\mathbf{O}^{-1})^{T}\\\mathbf{q}^T \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{h}^T \mathbf{x}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]

Classical derivation of far-field elastic x-ray scattering under the first Born approximation

Richard A. Kirian
(December 26, 2022)

1 Overview

There are multiple ways to develop the theory of x-ray diffraction. In order to make this guide accessible to motivated undergraduates, we will build upon the most popular undergraduate electrodynamics textbook by David Griffiths[1]. The main result we derive is the diffraction intensity equation:

I⁢(𝒒)=J0⁢re2⁢Δ⁢Ω⁢𝒫⁢(𝒒)⁢|∫ρ⁢(𝒓)⁢e−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓⁢d3⁢r|2. (1)

The above result assumes (1) perfect plane-wave illumination with wavelength λ, (2) far-field measurements (the distance to the detector is much greater than the size of the object) and (3) the first-order Born approximation, which loosely means that the scattering amplitudes are very weak compared to the incident amplitudes. The quantities in equation 1 are as follows:

  • •

    I⁢(𝒒) is the x-ray diffraction signal in a pixel (power per pixel)

  • •

    𝒒=𝒌−𝒌0 is the wavevector transfer

  • •

    J0 is the incident intensity (power per area)

  • •

    re=2.818×10−15 m is the Classical electron radius

  • •

    𝒌0 is the incident wavevector with magnitude |𝒌0|=|𝒌|=2⁢πλ

  • •

    𝒌 is the outgoing wavevector that points from the target to the detector pixel

  • •

    Δ⁢Ω is the solid angle of the detector pixel (assumed very small)

  • •

    𝒫⁢(𝒒) is the polarization factor defined below

  • •

    ρ⁢(𝒓) is the scattering density, which is approximately equal to the electron density.

2 Electromagnetic fields from a moving point charge

Here we briefly outline how the electromagnetic fields from a moving point charge were derived by Griffiths, starting with Maxwell’s equations in vacuum:

∇⋅𝑬 =ρ/ϵ0 ∇×𝑬 =−∂𝑩∂t (2)
∇⋅𝑩 =0 ∇×𝑩 =μ0⁢𝑱+μ0⁢ϵ0⁢∂𝑬∂t. (3)

As developed in Griffiths chapter 10, Maxwell’s equations may be written in terms of the scalar and vector potentials that are defined as

𝑩 =∇×𝑨, 𝑬 =−∇V−∂𝑨∂t. (4)

When working in the Lorenz gauge, namely

∇⋅𝑨=−μ0⁢ϵ0⁢∂V∂t, (5)

Maxwell’s equations are reduced to a set of four inhomogeneous wave equations:

∇2V−μ0⁢ϵ0⁢∂2V∂t2 =−ρ/ϵ0 ∇2𝑨−μ0⁢ϵ0⁢∂2𝑨∂t2 =−μ0⁢𝑱. (6)

The inhomogeneous wave equations may be solved using Green’s method111See Appendix A., which yields the retarded potentials:

V⁢(𝒓,t) =14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢∫ρ⁢(𝒓′,tr)R⁢d3⁢r′ 𝑨⁢(𝒓,t) =μ04⁢π⁢∫𝑱⁢(𝒓′,tr)R⁢d3⁢r′ (7)

where 𝑹=𝒓−𝒓′⁢(tr) and the retarded time is tr=t−R/c. For a point charge moving on the trajectory 𝒓′⁢(t), the retarded potentials become the Liénard-Wiechert potentials222See Appenxix B:

V⁢(𝒓,t) =14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢q|R−𝑹⋅𝜷| 𝑨⁢(𝒓,t) =μ04⁢π⁢q⁢𝒗|R−𝑹⋅𝜷|, (8)

where 𝜷=𝒗/c=𝒓˙′⁢(tr)/c. From the Liénard-Wiechert potentials, Griffiths used equation 4 work out the fields for a point charge:

𝑬⁢(𝒓,t)=q4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢R(𝑹⋅𝒖)3⁢[(c2−v2)⁢𝒖+𝑹×(𝒖×𝒂)],𝑩⁢(𝒓,t)=1c⁢𝑹^×𝑬⁢(𝒓,t) (9)

where 𝒖=c⁢𝑹^−𝜷 and 𝒂=𝒓¨′⁢(tr). Importantly, these fields are correct for relativistic particles, and as such they may be used to derive both the x-ray scattering amplitudes as well as the incident source fields created by x-ray free-electron lasers (XFELs) and synchrotrons.

3 Dipole radiation

Under the dipole approximations we consider locations very far from a localized source. The electric field contains two terms: the generalized Coulomb field and the radiation field:

𝑬⁢(𝒓,t)=𝑬Coulomb⁢(𝒓,t)+𝑬rad⁢(𝒓,t). (10)

The radiation field in equation 9 dominates because it falls off in proportion to 1/R whereas the generalized Coulomb field falls off in proportion to 1/R2. We therefore focus on the radiation field

𝑬rad⁢(𝒓,t)=q4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢R(𝑹⋅𝒖)3⁢[𝑹×(𝒖×𝒂)],𝑩rad⁢(𝒓,t)=1c⁢𝑹^×𝑬⁢(𝒓,t) (11)

for the remainder of this note.

Consider a point charge that oscillates about the origin with position 𝒓′=r0⁢exp⁡(−i⁢ω⁢t)⁢𝒓^′ (we take the real part of this). We make the standard dipole radiation assumptions: the dipole is small compared to the distance to our observation point, which means that r≫r′ and hence 𝑹≈𝒓. We further assume that the charged particle is non-relativistic, which means that333Equivalently, r0⁢ω/c=2⁢π⁢r0/λ≪1; the wavelength is much larger than the dipole, and the time it takes light to traverse the extent of the dipole is small compared to the period of oscillation. v≪c, and we may use 𝒖≈c⁢𝑹^. Plugging these in, we get

𝑬dip⁢(𝒓,t) =q4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢r(𝒓⋅c⁢𝒓^)3⁢𝒓×(c⁢𝒓^×𝒂) (12)
=μ04⁢π⁢1r⁢𝒓^×(𝒓^×𝒑¨⁢(tr)) (13)

where 𝒑⁢(t)=q⁢𝒓′⁢(t) is the dipole moment of the point charge. The magnetic field is

𝑩dip=1c⁢𝒓^×𝑬dip (14)

and the Poynting vector is

𝑺=1μ0⁢𝑬dip×𝑩dip=1μ0⁢c⁢Edip2⁢𝒓^=μ016⁢π2⁢c⁢r2⁢|𝒓^×𝒑¨|2⁢𝒓^. (15)

The differential power radiated through a differential area d⁢𝒂=r2⁢sin⁡θ⁢d⁢θ⁢d⁢ϕ⁢𝒓^=r2⁢d⁢Ω⁢𝒓^ is

d⁢P=𝑺⋅d⁢𝒂=μ016⁢π2⁢c⁢|𝒓^×𝒑¨|2⁢d⁢Ω. (17)

The total power radiated away is

P=∫d⁢Pd⁢Ω⁢𝑑Ω=μ016⁢π2⁢c⁢∫|𝒓^×𝒑¨|2⁢𝑑Ω. (18)

The above integral does not depend on the orientation of the coordinate system, and is most easily performed if we choose |𝒓^×𝒑^|2=sin2⁡θ which yields a factor of 8⁢π/3. The final result is the Larmor formula:

P=μ0⁢p¨26⁢π⁢c. (19)

⟨d⁢P⟩ =μ0⁢ω4⁢p232⁢π2⁢c⁢|𝒓^×𝒑^|2⁢d⁢Ω (20)

4 Thompson scatter from a free electron

An example of a very simple classical scattering process is a free electron exposed to an electromagnetic wave, which results in acceleration and hence re-radiation or scattering. Unfortunately, this is not covered in Griffiths. The force on an electron (mass me and charge e) due to an incident light beam with oscillating electric field 𝑬0⁢(t) results in the acceleration

𝒂⁢(t)=−eme⁢𝑬0⁢(t). (22)

If the electron was bound to an atom we could develop a crude model by adding a restoring force and damping force as in Griffiths chapter 9. In our simple model for a free electron, the second derivative of the dipole moment is

𝒑¨=−e⁢𝒂=e2me⁢𝑬0⁢(t). (23)

Plugging this into the dipole radiation formula 12 gives

𝑬dip=−rer⁢[𝒓^×(𝒓^×𝑬0⁢(tr))] (24)

where we simplified the expression by defining the classical electron radius

re=14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢e2me⁢c2≈2.8×10−15⁢m. (25)

The Poynting vector is

𝑺=1μ0⁢𝑬dip×𝑩dip=1μ0⁢c⁢Edip2⁢𝒓^=μ016⁢π2⁢c⁢r2⁢|𝒓^×𝒑¨|2⁢𝒓^. (26)

We therefore have the power captured by a detector pixel:

⟨d⁢P⟩=re2⁢E022⁢μ0⁢c⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^0|2⁢d⁢Ω=I0⁢re2⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^0|2⁢d⁢Ω. (27)

where I0=|𝑬^0|2/2⁢μ0⁢c is the incident radiation intensity. Integrating over all angles we get

P=83⁢π⁢re2⁢I0=σT⁢I0 (28)

where σT is the Thompson scattering cross section.

5 Polarization Factor

The “polarization factor” is the term 𝒫=|𝒓^×𝑬^0|2=sin2⁡θ in equation 27, which corresponds to linear polarization along the direction 𝑬^0. We may generalize to an arbitrary elliptical polarization if we write the field as a superposition of two orthogonal polarizations:

𝑬0=𝑬1+ei⁢ϕ⁢𝑬2 (29)

where 𝑬^2=𝒌^0×𝑬^1. The time-averaged Poynting vector becomes444See Appendix C for details.

⟨𝑺⁢(𝒓)⟩ =re2⁢1r2⁢(I1⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^1|2+I2⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^2|2)⁢𝒓^. (30)

If we write the overall incident intensity as

I0=I1+I2 (31)

and parameterize the intensity components according to the polarization power weight α such that

I1 =α⁢I0 (32)
I2 =(1−α)⁢I0 (33)

we may write the generalization of equation 27:

⟨d⁢P⟩=I0⁢re2⁢(α⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^1|2+(1−α)⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^2|2)⁢d⁢Ω. (34)

We typically work in terms of the fluence (time-integrated intensity) J0, which gives the expression for the measured diffraction signal555The units here are either energy or photons per pixel, depending on the units chosen for J0. Photons are often the preferred unit since the noise characteristics often depend on photon counts.

I⁢(𝒒)=J0⁢re2⁢𝒫⁢(𝒒)⁢Δ⁢Ω (35)

where the polarization factor is defined as

𝒫⁢(𝒒)=α⁢|𝒌^×𝑬^1|2+(1−α)⁢|𝒌^×𝑬^2|2 (36)

with the understanding that

𝒓^=𝒌^=𝒒−𝒌0|𝒒−𝒌0|. (37)

A convenient form for calculations666The use of dot products rather than angles is preferred since angles are often defined in different ways by different textbooks and software packages. Dot products are invariant to the usual coordinate transformations. is

𝒫⁢(𝒒) =1−α⁢(𝒌^⋅𝑬^1)2−(1−α)⁢(𝒌^⋅𝑬^2)2. (38)

6 Weak Diffraction (Born approximation)

Consider the the scattered field from a free electron located at position 𝒓′ under the influence of a plane wave

𝑬0⁢(𝒓,t)=𝑬0⁢exp⁡(i⁢𝒌0⋅𝒓−i⁢ω⁢t) (39)

The scattered field is

𝑬dip⁢(𝒓,t)=rer⁢[𝒓^×(𝒓^×𝑬0)]⁢exp⁡(i⁢𝒌0⋅𝒓′−i⁢ω⁢tr) (40)

where tr=t−R/c. Since we observe in the “far-field”, where r≫r′, we may use the following approximation:

R=(𝒓−𝒓′)2=r2+r′⁣2−2⁢𝒓⋅𝒓′≈r−𝒓^⋅𝒓′. (41)

Plugging in the approximation tr≈t−r/c+𝒓^⋅𝒓′ we have

𝑬dip⁢(𝒓,t)=rer⁢[𝒓^×(𝒓^×𝑬0)]⁢exp⁡(i⁢𝒌0⋅𝒓′−i⁢ω⁢t+i⁢ωc⁢(r−𝒓^⋅𝒓′)). (42)

Defining 𝒌=ω/c⁢𝒓^=2⁢π/λ⁢𝒓^ (the outgoing wavevector, directed at the detector) and 𝒒=𝒌−𝒌0, we get

𝑬dip⁢(𝒓,t)=re⁢ei⁢k⁢rr⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢[𝒓^×(𝒓^×𝑬0)]⁢e−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓′. (43)

Finally, in order to form a diffraction pattern, we sum over many electrons at positions 𝒓i′:

𝑬diff⁢(𝒓,t)=re⁢ei⁢k⁢rr⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢[𝒓^×(𝒓^×𝑬0)]⁢∑ie−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓i′. (44)

In the continuum limit, with electron density ρ⁢(𝒓), we have

𝑬diff⁢(𝒓,t)=re⁢ei⁢k⁢rr⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢[𝒓^×(𝒓^×𝑬0)]⁢∫ρ⁢(𝒓′)⁢e−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓′⁢d3⁢r′. (45)

We typically define the “form factor” as

F⁢(𝒒)=∫ρ⁢(𝒓′)⁢e−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓′⁢d3⁢r′. (46)

This form factor is just the Fourier transform of the electron density, with the conjugate variable 𝒒 being defined by the incoming and outgoing wavevectors. The time-averaged Poynting vector is777See Appendix C for details.

⟨𝑺⁢(𝒓)⟩=1μ0⁢⟨ℜ⁡{𝑬diff}×ℜ⁡{𝑩diff}⟩=re2⁢1r2⁢J0⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^0|2⁢|∫ρ⁢(𝒓′)⁢e−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓′⁢d3⁢r′|2⁢𝒓^. (47)

Where we have used the incident intensity J0=|𝑬0|2/2⁢μ0⁢c. Assuming a detector pixel with area d⁢𝒂=r2⁢d⁢Ω⁢𝒓^, the power into the detector pixel is

I⁢(𝒒)=⟨𝑺⁢(𝒓)⟩⋅d⁢𝒂=re2⁢J0⁢|𝒓^×𝑬^0|2⁢|∫ρ⁢(𝒓′)⁢e−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓′⁢d3⁢r′|2⁢d⁢Ω. (48)

Generalizing the polarization factor yields equation 1:

I⁢(𝒒)=J0⁢re2⁢𝒫⁢(𝒒)⁢|∫ρ⁢(𝒓′)⁢e−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓′⁢d3⁢r′|2⁢Δ⁢Ω. (49)

The only difference between a single point-like electron (equation 34) and a charge distribution (the above equation) is the appearance of the form factor F⁢(𝒒).

7 Anomalous Dispersion

A simple classical model for “anomalous dispersion” was developed in Griffiths chapter 9, as follows. Instead of the free electron we considered in the previous sections, we allow for a restoring force and a damping force in addition to the driving force of the external field. This model gets us closer to one that includes the affects of the atom to which the electrons are bound. The position 𝒙 of the electron obeys the following differential equation:

me⁢d2⁢𝒙d⁢t2+me⁢γ⁢d⁢𝒙d⁢t+me⁢ω02⁢𝒙=e⁢𝑬0⁢exp⁡(−i⁢ω⁢t). (50)

The solution for 𝒙⁢(t) gives the dipole moment

𝒑⁢(t)=e⁢𝒙⁢(t)=e2/meω02−ω2−i⁢γ⁢ω⁢𝑬0⁢exp⁡(−i⁢ω⁢t). (51)

When far from resonance, the simplest model for an atomic scattering factor is to assume the atom has an electron density ρn(0)⁢(r) and calculate the form factor in equation 46. For a rotationally symmetric atom888Most tabulations of scattering factors use a rotational symmetry approximation, and it is very rare to image with resolutions that can resolve internal atomic structure. situated at the origin, the atomic scattering factor is defined as999Appendix D derives 53.

fn(0)⁢(q)=∫−∞∞ρn(0)⁢(r)⁢exp⁡(−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓)⁢d3⁢r=∫0∞ρn(0)⁢(r)⁢sin⁡(q⁢r)q⁢r⁢4⁢π⁢r2⁢𝑑r. (53)

For an atom located at the position 𝒓n, the scattering factor takes on the phase shift

fn(0)⁢(𝒒)=∫−∞∞ρn⁢(|𝒓−𝒓n|)⁢exp⁡(−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓)⁢d3⁢r=fn(0)⁢(q)⁢exp⁡(−i⁢𝒒⋅𝒓n). (54)

A very common way of accounting for anomalous dispersion in atomic scattering factors is to include real and imaginary energy-dependent “dispersion correction” terms to the scattering factors:

9 Refractive Index

Starting at equation 51 and assuming we have a number density N of such “atoms”, the polarization (dipole moment per volume) is

𝑷⁢(t)=N⁢e2/meω02−ω2−i⁢γ⁢ω⁢𝑬0⁢exp⁡(−i⁢ω⁢t). (58)

Using the usual definitions for a linear material, 𝑷=ϵ0⁢χe⁢𝑬, ϵ=ϵ0⁢(1+χe), and n=ϵ/ϵ0, we arrive at the refractive index

n=1+N⁢e2/me⁢ϵ0ω02−ω2−i⁢γ⁢ω (59)

In the high-frequency limit, we have

n≈1−N⁢e22⁢m⁢ϵ0⁢ω2. (60)

The above is usually written in terms of the classical electron radius re:

n≈1−12⁢π⁢re⁢N⁢λ2. (61)

Note that the refractive index for x-rays is usually very close to 1, but slightly less than 1. We may relate the index of refraction to the scattering density from equation 57:

n⁢(𝒓)≈1−12⁢π⁢re⁢ρ⁢(𝒓)⁢λ2. (62)

Appendix A Appendix: Solution to the inhomogeneous wave equation

We wish to solve for V⁢(𝒓,t) in the inhomogeneous equation

∇2V⁢(𝒓,t)−μ0⁢ϵ0⁢∂2V⁢(𝒓,t)∂t2 =−ρ⁢(𝒓,t)/ϵ0 (63)

assuming that we know ρ⁢(𝒓,t). Note that if we solve this equation, the same approach may be applied to the components of the vector potential 𝑨 in equation 6.

Briefly, Green’s method works as follows. Assume we have a linear operator ℒ𝒓 and we wish to solve for V⁢(𝒓) in the following equation:

ℒ𝒓⁢V⁢(𝒓)=g⁢(𝒓). (64)

The 𝒓 subscript indicates that the operator involves the unprimed coordinates. Now assume further that we can find the “Green’s function” G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′) that satisfies the equation

ℒ𝒓⁢G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)=δ⁢(𝒓−𝒓′) (65)

where δ⁢(𝒓−𝒓′) is the Dirac delta function. Once equipped with G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′) we may easily show that the solution for V⁢(𝒓) is

V⁢(𝒓)=∫Volg⁢(𝒓′)⁢G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)⁢d3⁢r′. (66)

Working the proof backwards from the above, we apply ℒ𝒓 to both sides of the equation:

ℒ𝒓⁢V⁢(𝒓) =ℒ𝒓⁢∫Volg⁢(𝒓′)⁢G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)⁢d3⁢r′ (67)
=∫Volg⁢(𝒓′)⁢ℒ𝒓⁢G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)⁢d3⁢r′ (68)
=∫Volg⁢(𝒓′)⁢δ⁢(𝒓−𝒓′)⁢d3⁢r′ (69)
ℒ𝒓⁢V⁢(𝒓) =g⁢(𝒓) (70)

In order to solve equation 63, we will first remove the time variable by expanding V⁢(𝒓) and ρ⁢(𝒓) into their Fourier transforms:

V⁢(𝒓,t) =∫−∞∞Vω⁢(𝒓)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑ω (71)
ρ⁢(𝒓,t) =∫−∞∞ρω⁢(𝒓)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑ω. (72)

We plug the above into equation 63 we get a differential equation for each spectral component:

(∇2+k2)⁢Vω⁢(𝒓)=−ρω⁢(𝒓)/ϵ0 (73)

where k=μ0⁢ϵ0⁢ω. We now seek the Green’s function for

(∇2+k2)⁢G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)=δ⁢(𝒓−𝒓′) (74)

The above is easiest to solve if the delta function is at the origin, so we define 𝑹=𝒓−𝒓′:

(∇2+k2)⁢G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)=δ⁢(𝑹) (75)

When written in terms of the 𝑹 coordinates, the RHS is rotationally invariant, and hence the Green’s function can only be a function of the radial magnitude: G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)=G⁢(R). In writing the Laplacian operator in spherical coordinates, we’ve reduced our equation to a single variable:

(1R⁢∂∂R⁢R+k2)⁢G⁢(R)=δ⁢(R). (76)

If we look in the regions R≠0 where δ⁢(R)=0 we have the equation

∂∂R⁢[R⁢G⁢(R)]=−k2⁢[R⁢G⁢(R)] (77)

with the usual solutions

G⁢(R)=A⁢ei⁢k⁢RR+B⁢e−i⁢k⁢RR. (78)

With foresight, we can set B=0 because the incoming spherical waves produce results that violate causality (we’ve effectively introduced a boundary condition at infinity). In order to determine the coefficient A we eliminate the Delta function by taking the volume integral of both sides of equation 76 about an infinitesimal volume centered on the origin:

∫sphere∇2G⁢(R)⁢d3⁢R+k2⁢∫sphereG⁢(R)⁢d3⁢R=1. (79)

In the limit R→0 we have G⁢(R)→A/R:

A⁢∫sphere∇21R⁢d3⁢R+A⁢k2⁢∫sphere1R⁢4⁢π⁢R2⁢𝑑R=1. (80)

For the first term on the LHS we use the result from Griffiths chapter 1: ∇2(1/R)=−4⁢π⁢δ⁢(R). The second term on the LHS vanishes. Finally giving us

A=−14⁢π. (81)

After all the above work, we finally have the Green’s function – an outgoing spherical wave:

G⁢(𝒓,𝒓′)=−ei⁢k⁢|𝒓−𝒓′|4⁢π⁢|𝒓−𝒓′|. (82)

With the Green’s function, we finally plug it into equation 66:

Vω⁢(𝒓)=∫Volρω⁢(𝒓′)4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢ei⁢k⁢|𝒓−𝒓′||𝒓−𝒓′|⁢d3⁢r′. (83)

Finally, we switch from frequency to the time variable by taking a Fourier transform of the above:

V⁢(𝒓,t) =∫−∞∞{∫Volρω⁢(𝒓′)4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢ei⁢k⁢|𝒓−𝒓′||𝒓−𝒓′|⁢d3⁢r′}⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t⁢𝑑ω (84)
=∫Vol{∫−∞∞ρω⁢(𝒓′)⁢e−i⁢ω⁢(t−|𝒓−𝒓′|/c)⁢𝑑ω}⁢14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢1|𝒓−𝒓′|⁢d3⁢r′. (85)

Comparing the term in the curly braces with equation 71 gives us the retarded potential

V⁢(𝒓,t) =14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢∫ρ⁢(𝒓′,tr)R⁢d3⁢r′ (86)

along with the retarded time

tr≡t−R/c. (87)

Appendix B Appendix: Liénard-Wiechert potential

We have a particle moving along the trajectory 𝒘⁢(t), and we want the retarded potential at position 𝒓:

V⁢(𝒓,t)=14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢∫ρ⁢(𝒓′,tr)|𝒓−𝒓′|⁢d3⁢r′ (88)

where the retarded time is

tr⁢(t,𝒓′)≡t−|𝒓−𝒓′|/c (89)

The density of a point particle is

ρ⁢(𝒓,t) =q⁢δ⁢(𝒓−𝒘⁢(t)) (90)
=q⁢∫δ⁢(𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′))⁢δ⁢(t′−tr⁢(t,𝒓′))⁢𝑑t′ (91)

Plug the density into the retarded potential:

V⁢(𝒓,t)=q4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢∬δ⁢(𝒓′−𝒘⁢(t′))⁢δ⁢(t′−tr⁢(t,𝒓′))|𝒓−𝒓′|⁢d3⁢r′⁢𝑑t′ (92)

Note that the time t that appears in the retarded time is not the same as the dummy variable t′. Now we do the integral over space, which involves swapping 𝒓′ with 𝒘⁢(t) everywhere. In particular, note that there is an 𝒓′ in the retarded time tr⁢(t,𝒓′)

V⁢(𝒓,t)=q4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢∫δ⁢(t′−tr⁢(t,𝒘⁢(t′)))|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|⁢𝑑t′ (93)

Next we need to do the integral over time, but we have a delta function with a function as its argument. The general rule for a delta function is that it is zero except when the argument is equal to zero. To handle this situation, we use the following general rule:

δ⁢(g⁢(x)) =δ⁢(x−x′)|g′⁢(x′)| (94)

where x′ is implicitly defined by the expression

g⁢(x′) =0 (95)

and the primed g′⁢(x) means to take the derivative

g′⁢(x) ≡dd⁢x⁢g⁢(x). (96)

We therefore can write our delta function in time as:

δ⁢(t′−tr⁢(t,𝒘⁢(t′)))=δ⁢(t′−t*)|dd⁢t′⁢(t′−(t−|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|c))t′=t*| (97)

We work out the denominator:

dd⁢t′⁢(t′−(t−|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|c)) =1+1c⁢dd⁢t′⁢|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)| (98)
=1+1c⁢dd⁢t′⁢((𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′))2)1/2 (99)
=1+12⁢c⁢1|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|⁢dd⁢t′⁢(𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′))2 (100)
=1+12⁢c⁢1|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|⁢dd⁢t′⁢(𝒓⋅𝒓+𝒘⁢(t′)⋅𝒘⁢(t′)−2⁢𝒓⋅𝒘⁢(t′)) (101)
=1+12⁢c⁢1|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|⁢(2⁢𝒘⁢(t′)⋅𝒘˙⁢(t′)−2⁢𝒓⋅𝒘˙⁢(t′)) (102)
=1−1c⁢(𝒓⋅𝒘˙⁢(t′)−𝒘⁢(t′)⋅𝒘˙⁢(t′))|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)| (103)
=1−1c⁢(𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′))⋅𝒘˙⁢(t′)|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)| (104)

We now determine what t* is by the implicit expression

t′−(t−|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|c)=0 (105)

Compare the above to the definition of the retarded time of the particle:

tr=t−|𝒓−𝒘⁢(tr)|c (106)

Thus we have t*=tr, which we substitute in to get

V⁢(𝒓,t) =q4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢∫1|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)|⁢δ⁢(t′−tr)|1−1c⁢(𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′))⋅𝒘˙⁢(t′)|𝒓−𝒘⁢(t′)||⁢𝑑t′ (107)
=q4⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢1|𝒓−𝒘⁢(tr)|⁢1|1−1c⁢(𝒓−𝒘⁢(tr))⋅𝒘˙⁢(tr)|𝒓−𝒘⁢(tr)|| (108)

We can now clean this up with the definitions 𝒘˙⁢(tr)=𝒗, 𝜷=𝒗/c and R=𝒓−𝒘⁢(tr):

V⁢(𝒓,t)=14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢q|R−𝑹⋅𝜷| (109)

This same procedure may be applied to each of the components of 𝑨 to get

𝑨⁢(𝒓,t)=14⁢π⁢ϵ0⁢q⁢𝒗|R−𝑹⋅𝜷|. (110)

Appendix C Appendix: Time-averaged Poynting vector

Supposing that we have the electric field 𝑬⁢(t)=𝑬0⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t and corresponding magnetic field 𝑩⁢(t)=𝑩0⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t=1c⁢𝒌^×𝑬0⁢e−i⁢ω⁢t, and we want the time averaged Poynting vector

⟨𝑺⟩=1T⁢∫0T1μ0⁢ℜ⁡{𝑬}×ℜ⁡{𝑩}⁢𝑑t, (111)

we must be careful to take the real part of the complex fields. We can write the real part as

ℜ⁡{𝑬}=12⁢(𝑬+𝑬*) (112)

and plug this into the above:

⟨𝑺⟩=1T⁢∫0T1μ0⁢12⁢(𝑬+𝑬*)×12⁢(𝑩+𝑩*)⁢𝑑t. (113)

Importantly, the cross terms involving 𝑬×𝑩 and 𝑬*×𝑩* will average to zero because of the time dependent term e−2⁢i⁢ω⁢t that remains in the integral. In the other two terms, there is no time dependence, and therefore

⟨𝑺⟩=1μ0⁢14⁢(𝑬×𝑩*+𝑬*×𝑩)=12⁢μ0⁢ℜ⁡{𝑬×𝑩*}. (114)

In vacuum we have 𝑩=1c⁢𝒌^×𝑬 so that

⟨𝑺⟩=12⁢c⁢μ0⁢ℜ⁡{𝑬×(𝒌^×𝑬*)}=12⁢c⁢μ0⁢|E|2⁢𝒌^=I⁢𝒌^. (115)

If the E field has two orthogonal components 𝑬=𝑬1+𝑬2 such that 𝑬1⋅𝑬2=0 and 𝑩=1c⁢𝒌^×𝑬 then

⟨𝑺⟩=12⁢c⁢μ0⁢ℜ⁡{(𝑬1+𝑬2)×(𝒌^×(𝑬1+𝑬2)*)}. (116)

Due to the orthogonality of the field components, the cross terms vanish: e.g.

𝑬1×(𝒌^×𝑬2*)=0. (117)

Therefore the average Poynting vector is

⟨𝑺⟩=12⁢c⁢μ0⁢(|𝑬1|2+|𝑬2|2)⁢𝒌^. (118)
